Abstract

High-Definition Maps (HD Maps) are essential for both Advanced Driver-Assistance Systems (ADAS) and autonomous vehicles. Offline HD map construction is costly and difficult to maintain due to the dynamic nature of real-world driving environments. Consequently, online HD map generation using onboard sensors has become a focus of the autonomous driving community. However, current deep learning-based methods often rely on computationally intensive feature extractors, hindering their practical deployment. In this paper, we propose eTLSD, an efficient end-to-end neural network that generates rich HD maps containing both topological and geometric road information. To achieve this efficiency and improved performance, we introduce four key innovations: (1) an iterative refinement scheme within the decoder; (2) a group-wise one-to-many assignment strategy for faster convergence of the map learning network; (3) a novel module incorporating lane segment coordinates using a graph neural network; and (4) a distance-aware topological reasoning post-processing method to enhance the quality of connectivity outputs. Evaluations on the OpenLane-V2 dataset demonstrate that eTLSD achieves an average OLUS performance improvement of 6.7% (6.4% on mAP and 14.5% on TOP_lsls metric) compared to existing methods while utilizing a significantly lighter ResNet-18 backbone instead of the commonly employed ResNet-50. This enhanced performance with a smaller model facilitates easier deployment.

Method

Overview of the TLSD framework. Multi-view images are processed by a 2D backbone, converted into a bird’s-eye view (BEV) with BEVFormer, and decoded with a transformer. TLSD enhances this pipeline using grouped queries, iterative topology refinement, graph-based reasoning, and a distance-aware post-processing step.

Problem Formulation

The goal is to detect all lane segments from multi-view cameras and establish how they connect to each other in a directed graph representation.

Group-wise One-to-Many Assignment

Queries are divided into groups so that each group covers multiple matches. This increases supervision, stabilizes training, and speeds up convergence without adding extra decoder complexity.

Topology Iterative Refinement

The connectivity between lane segments is refined layer by layer in the decoder, ensuring early predictions are topology-aware and later layers polish the details.

Graph Reasoning on Lane Coordinates

Lane coordinates are processed with a graph neural network that exchanges information among neighboring segments. This adds spatial priors and improves the accuracy of connectivity predictions.

Distance-Aware Post-Processing (DAPP)

Finally, a distance-based adjustment is applied to connectivity scores during inference, balancing precision and stability in the final output.

Evaluation

Centerline Perception

Evaluation on lane centerline perception using the OpenLane-V2 benchmark. Metrics marked with * are extracted from the official TOP_ll v1.0.0 results reported in prior work. The derived metric OLS∗ reflects a composite of DETl and TOP_ll. TLSD demonstrates state-of-the-art performance across training schedules, with our ResNet-18 variant achieving highly competitive results against deeper architectures.

Lane Segment Perception

Quantitative comparison on the OpenLane-V2 benchmark for lane segment perception. TLSD achieves state-of-the-art performance at both 24 and 48 training epochs. Notably, our lightweight ResNet-18 model competes closely with heavier ResNet-50 backbones, demonstrating strong efficiency-accuracy tradeoffs.

Qualitative examples comparing TLSD with prior approaches. These visualizations highlight TLSD’s superior ability to capture long-range dependencies and preserve lane connectivity.
